Overview

The Problem

A Fortune 500 company with 5,000+ employees was experiencing alarming rates of digital tool abandonment, IT ticket volume, and reported burnout linked to internal software. Exit interviews consistently cited "the systems" as a source of daily stress.

This wasn't a visual design problem. It was a behavioral systems problem — and no one had approached it that way yet.

Research

Diagnosing the Cognitive Load

I designed and ran a mixed-methods research program: 24 contextual interviews, a 500-person survey using a validated technostress scale, and 40+ hours of observational sessions across 5 departments. The findings pointed to 4 core behavioral failure modes: decision overload, attention fragmentation, forced context-switching, and absent feedback loops.

"By 10am, I've already made 200 micro-decisions in three different systems. I'm exhausted before my real work starts."

[ Behavioral failure mode map — 4 core patterns across 12 tools ]

Framework

The Behavioral UX Framework

I developed a proprietary evaluation and design framework with 5 dimensions: Cognitive Load Index, Decision Density, Feedback Quality, Context Coherence, and Recovery Cost. Each dimension had measurable criteria and corresponding design interventions.

The framework gave the internal design team a repeatable methodology — something they could apply to any new or existing tool without needing a behavioral researcher in the room.

[ Framework diagram — 5 dimensions with scoring rubrics ]

Implementation

Applying the Framework

We piloted across 3 high-usage internal tools, redesigning specific interaction patterns — not entire UIs. Targeted interventions: progressive disclosure to reduce decision overload, persistent context panels to eliminate context-switching, and meaningful micro-feedback to close cognitive loops.

[ Before/after interaction patterns — progressive disclosure pilot ]

Outcome

Results & Adoption

After a 12-week rollout, error rates dropped 48%. Task completion improved 2.1×. Self-reported technostress decreased 40%. The framework was adopted as company-wide design standard and is now used across 14 internal tools. The VP of Engineering called it "the most impactful design initiative in 5 years."
